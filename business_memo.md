# Business Memo: Pre-Retention Investigation Priorities

## Context
Before launching a retention campaign, the company should first validate which behavioral patterns are most associated with churn in the current customer base. The Part 1 analysis is designed to move from raw data inspection to practical churn-risk hypotheses using customer profile, order behavior, support interactions, web engagement, and campaign history [file:1][file:340].

## Main concerns
Several patterns deserve immediate investigation before spending retention budget broadly. The draft analysis specifically focuses on recency, support-ticket volume, return behavior, web activity, and discount usage as possible churn indicators, which are all plausible commercial risk signals for a D2C brand [file:340].

The company should investigate whether customers with longer gaps since last purchase, repeated support issues, high return rates, and low recent digital engagement are disproportionately represented in the churn label set [file:340]. These patterns are more actionable than generic churn summaries because they can be tied to specific interventions such as service recovery, win-back campaigns, or experience fixes [file:1][file:340].

## Recommended investigation areas
### 1. Purchase inactivity
Customers who have not purchased recently may represent the clearest and most direct churn-risk pool, especially if recency differences align strongly with churn in the notebook outputs [file:340]. This group should be analyzed first because recency is operationally easy to measure and often supports fast campaign targeting.

### 2. Support friction
Repeated tickets or slow resolution may indicate broken customer experience, not just low engagement [file:340]. If churn is elevated among customers with multiple tickets or poor ticket sentiment, retention action should focus on problem resolution and service recovery rather than discounts alone.

### 3. Return and dissatisfaction signals
A high return rate may indicate product mismatch, fulfillment issues, or expectation gaps [file:340]. If high-return customers also churn at elevated rates, the brand should review quality control, product education, shade/fit guidance, and return-reason analysis before scaling promotional offers.

### 4. Digital disengagement
Low sessions and long time since last visit may signal silent churn risk even before the customer stops ordering entirely [file:340]. This group is suitable for low-cost reactivation experiments such as personalized reminders, category education, or browse-abandon nudges.

### 5. Price sensitivity
Heavy historical discount usage may reveal customers who buy only under promotion pressure [file:340]. The business should separate genuinely loyal customers from promotion-dependent customers so that margin-destructive blanket discounting is avoided.

## Campaign caution
The company should not launch a broad retention campaign using every available customer equally because the capstone objective explicitly warns against blind discounting and encourages targeted, data-backed action [file:1]. A better approach is to validate a few high-signal churn hypotheses first, then prioritize customers whose churn risk is linked to identifiable and fixable behaviors [file:1][file:340].
